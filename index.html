STEEPLE Analysis

The STEEPLE Analysis comes from a tool called the PEST analysis. It was developed by Harvard Business Professor Francis J. Aguilar, in his 1964 book Scanning the Business Environment. The main idea behind this process is to get a general impression of the community where you will be evaluating public meetings. We are using a version of the tool from management training firm Professional Academy, which asks questions about the Social, Technical, Environmental, Economic, Political, Legal, and Ethical characteristics of the local community. The letters have been rearranged to STEEPLE so it is easier to remember and also organizes related concepts in a more logical order (example: political next to legal).

Social
  1. List the name of the community where your team will evaluate the local public meetings:
  2. Tell Us About Your Community:
    • For this question, we ask that you use Quickfacts from the US Census to learn about your community. Use this link. You can search by town or city name, or zip code. Look over the factsheet, and then enter 3 to 5 important facts to know. (If you're not sure what to include, you might look at percentages about race, or age, or income level to start!)
  3. What are some current controversial political issues in your community?
Technical
  1. Do many people in your community lack high-speed internet access at home?
    • You can use the Census Quickfacts to help answer this!:
  2. Is it easy to find information about public meetings on your local government websites?
  3. Is public transportation available in your community?
Environmental
  1. Does weather ever make it hard to attend meetings?
  2. Are public meetings held at times and places accessible by public transportation?
Economic
  1. What percentage of people in your community live in poverty?
    • You can use Census QuickFacts to find the answer:
  2. What is your local government's budget?
    • If you find a budget document online, please include a link.
  3. List some of the main companies or industries where people in your community work:
Political
  1. What positions are elected in your community?
    • Who holds these positions?
  2. Who is the mayor?
    • You can write "n/a" if your community doesn't have one.
  3. Who are the county commissioners?
    • You can write "n/a" if your community doesn't have them.
  4. Are there other relevant officials who are not elected?
    • Examples: City manager, Sheriff.
  5. When are the next elections for your community?
  6. Do you know of other local groups advocating for open meetings?
Legal

You might want to know that public meetings in North Carolina are governed by Article 33C in the State Law, "Meetings of Public Bodies." If you want to understand the legal context of public meetings in North Carolina, we suggest "Open Meetings and Other Legal Requirements for Local Government Boards" by Frayda S. Bluestein at the UNC School of Government.

  1. Are there any additional laws related to public meetings in your city or council?
    • These laws would typically be found in the Code of Ordinances or in the bylaws for an individual board or commission.
  2. What actions can you take if a current policy is not being followed in practice?
  3. What is the process to propose changes to current policies?
Ethical
  1. Great work on the STEEPLE analysis! Take some time to look over what you have learned about the local community, so far. What characteristics does your team share with this community?
    • Examples: Race, economic background, ability, etc.
  3. Are there characteristics from your community that aren't represented by your local team?
  4. How can your local team make sure that marginalized voices are recognized?
  5. Have there been safety issues associated with public meetings in your community?
    • "Safety issues" could include threats to public officials or community members, or other ways that might make people scared to participate in public meetings.

Meeting Evaluation

Our meeting evaluation tools will be based on the following findings by Code for Asheville, Accessibility Beyond Compliance, as well as additional sources on usability:

During the creation of this policy proposal, the following challenges with public participation were identified:

  • The current public comment process requires a burdensome advance sign-up process that requires disclosure of unnecessary personal information.
  • Meeting materials do not contain a clear indication of updates or modifications to documents that were posted previously.
  • Local government entities lack data-driven metrics of public participation, such as the number of comments or the methods under which they were provided. As a result, it is challenging for staff, elected officials, or the public to analyze how changes to the public meeting process increase or decrease public participation.
  • Due to a lack of consistency in the process for public participation, it can be difficult to evaluate public support or opposition to measures considered by public bodies.

Before the Meeting

All Meetings

  1. What is the name of the public body holding the meeting? A public body could be a board, commission, council, or other group.
  2. What is the date of the meeting?
  3. A meeting agenda lists the topics to be discussed during a meeting. When was the meeting agenda available (allow only one selection):
    • At least one week in advance (7+ days before the meeting)
    • 1-6 days before the meeting
    • The day of the meeting
    • It was never available
  4. What logistical/practical information are you able to find (mark all that apply):
    • Meeting location/address
    • Meeting time
    • Parking information
    • Public transit information
    • Accessibility considerations that will be provided
    • Instructions on requesting additional accessibility measures
    • None of the above
  5. Before attending the meeting, which of the following, if any, were you able to locate? In this question, the word “board” refers to the board, commission, council, or other group holding the meeting (mark all that apply):
    • Board membership
    • Board contact info
    • Board meeting schedules
    • Board bylaws
    • Last time website was updated
    • None of the above
  6. Can you submit questions by voicemail or email before the meeting?
    • Yes, by voicemail only
    • Yes, by email only
    • Yes, by both email and voicemail
    • No
    • Unsure
    • You must submit questions some other way other than email or voicemail
  7. Does the meeting website incorporate any of the following digital accessibility considerations (mark all that apply):
    • Text is structured with tags to guide screen readers and help provide more context and information about the content of a webpage.
    • Alternative text (alt text) is provided for images and other non-text content
    • Color is used to complement information, rather than convey it completely
    • Contrast is used between text and background
    • Visual focus indicators are included to give a user guidance or draw attention to important content
    • Information is not hidden behind animations that make it difficult for users to navigate and focus (examples: autoplay videos, chat windows, pop-up boxes, or oversized animated cursors)
    • Other
    • Unsure
    • None of the above

During the Meeting

All Meetings

  • What is the process or meeting structure like?
    • If there's a schedule, list it out
  • Can the public participate (Y/N)?
    • If so, how?
  • How is it known what agenda items can and cannot be commented on by the public?
  • If any, which agenda items don't allow any comment or response from the public?

Online Only

  • Were there any disconnections that made you unable to comment/respond to participate in the meeting?
